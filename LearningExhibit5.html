Visualization and the Final Touches

I enjoyed this module as well. Playing with graphics was interesting but I am no artist or graphic designer. To be honest, I was kind of stumped by the typography exercise. I understand the basics about serif and sans-serif
fonts but beyond that I felt kind of confused. In terms of determining what fonts work well together, I just didn't get it. The whole thing seemed rather subjective to me. I can see that there is a real art to this and I
get that this is the kind of thing that graphic designers take very seriously (my wife has a graphic design degree, she tried to explain it to me but I still didn't get it), but in the end the whole thing was lost on me.


Inkscape and my Trouble with the Simple Things

I thought this was a pretty neat program and I coudn't believe that it was open source (I know that graphic editors can be very expensive). Right away I noticed how much more complex it is than paintbrush, a bit of a
problem for someone like myself, but I see how with this comes a lot more editing options and possibilities. My initial notes on this exercise are here.

As I said above, the problem I had with this exercise was rather silly and the solution to it was right in front of my face the whole time. The problem was with step 10: adding a legend. I just could not figure out how to
add text to the graph. I could see easily enough how to add the font, but to add the text was beyond me for quite a while. After clicking around and just starring at it for a while, I suddenly saw what it was that I had to do.
The answer was so simple and obvious I started to wonder if there was something wrong with me. Perhaps almost 2 months of hardly sleeping was finally catching up to me (newborn baby at home). All I had to do was
click the "add text objects" button on the control panel on the left-hand side of the screen. It was starring me in the face the whole time. Ohter than that everything went well, but in the end, for the graphics within my
final project, I stuck with Paint for the sake of simplicity. This is what I ended up with, and the file can be found here:




Typography

I had difficulty with this and, in the end, I do not think I ever really got it. Playign many round of Typeconnection made this very obvious to me. I didn't fail every time, but when I found a good match it was honestly
nothing but luck. I thin in total I had about three good matches out of at least ten tries. Not to mention, the fonts that I did match correctly were not available with Google Fonts. In the end, my font choices are the ones
you are seeing right now: Tinos sans-serif and Open-Sans-Condensed. They seem to me to work well together, but this of course is rather subjective and I could not really give a good reason why. The html file for the
exercise is here.


Conclusion

I don't think I would be a good graphic designer. I enjoyed working through these exercises and have respect for design professionals. I think this is the kind of thing that you either have or do not. Like many artistic
disciplines, aptitdue is everything. So, what I learned here about design is this: its tougher than it looks, and it is not something to be taken for granted.
